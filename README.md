# Coffee-Shop-Sales
☕ From 149,000+ transactions to 6 business decisions: my Coffee Shop Sales Analysis project

I recently completed an end-to-end retail sales analysis on a multi-location coffee shop business — and wanted to share not just the charts, but the actual business problems this kind of analysis solves.

The data: 149,116 transactions across 3 NYC store locations (Astoria, Hell's Kitchen, Lower Manhattan) over Jan–Jun 2023, totaling $698,812 in revenue.

Here's what the analysis answered — and why it matters:

📍 Problem 1: "Are we staffing the right hours?"
44% of daily revenue happens between 8–11 AM. The rest of the day is a flat plateau. This isn't a "slow day" business — it's a "slow hours" business. That completely changes how you schedule staff and prep inventory.

📍 Problem 2: "Is the business actually growing, or did we get lucky one month?"
Revenue grew 104% from January ($81.7K) to June ($166.5K), with consistent month-over-month acceleration from March onward. That's a trend worth protecting, not a fluke worth ignoring.

📍 Problem 3: "Is one of our stores underperforming?"
All three locations sit within 3% of each other in revenue. No location is silently bleeding the business — which means resources don't need to be diverted to "fix" a store that isn't actually broken.

📍 Problem 4: "What's our average customer actually worth?"
$4.69 per transaction. That single number becomes the baseline for testing every upsell or bundling idea going forward.

📍 Problem 5: "What do we never let go out of stock?"
Ethiopia, Jamaican Coffee River, Brazilian, and Columbian Medium Roast are the top performers by both volume AND revenue — the products the business cannot afford to run out of.

📍 Problem 6: "Where should marketing spend actually go?"
Coffee + Tea = 66.7% of total revenue. The rest (bakery, packaged goods, branded items) is a long tail of upsell opportunity sitting mostly untapped.

The bigger takeaway: good analysis isn't about pretty dashboards — it's about turning a spreadsheet into decisions someone can actually act on Monday morning.
